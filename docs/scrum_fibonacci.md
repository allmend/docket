# Story Points
## Why Use Fibonacci in Scrum?
- **Emphasizes Relative Sizing:** It is easier to agree that a task is "roughly a 5" than to argue whether it takes 5 or 6 hours.
- **Reflects Uncertainty:** The gaps between numbers increase as the tasks get bigger (e.g., jump from 13 to 21), highlighting that larger tasks are riskier and less predictable.
Fo**rces Decisions:** Using 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20 allows teams to decide if a task is closer to a 3 - or a 5, preventing the "average" estimate of 4, which often leads to picking the middle ground rather than evaluating complexity.
- **Speeds up Planning:** Because estimates are rougher, teams spend less time debating tiny differences in effort.

### Common Fibonacci Story Point Sequence
`0`, `0.5`, `1`, `2`, `3`, `5`, `8`, `13`, `20`, `40`, `100`

## Practical Application
- **Small Tasks (1-3):** Generally well-understood, low-risk tasks.
- **Medium Tasks (5-8):** Moderate complexity and uncertainty.
- **Large Tasks (13+):** High complexity/uncertainty; typically should be broken down into smaller stories.
- **Team Consensus:** During Planning Poker, if one team member estimates 13 and others estimate 3, it highlights a misunderstanding of the work, fostering critical discussion.

## Key Principles
- Points represent complexity and effort (CUE) — not hours.
- The scale is not linear; a 2 is not necessarily double the effort of a 1.
- Focus on comparing stories to a known benchmark (e.g., "This new story is roughly the same as that 5-point story we did last sprint").
